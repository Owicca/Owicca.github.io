Stages of stoicism:

  • Early:
    • Zeno of Citium(founder;330bce):
      • stoicism comes from the parto of the agora zeno and it’s followers would meet and discuss philosophy, Painted Stoa(covered colonnades), first named Zenonians, then named Stoics
      • influenced by cynics, plato, megarian
    • Aristo of Chios(pupil of zeno):
      • focused on ethics
      • eavily influenced by the cynics
    • Cleantes of Assos(turkey):
      • head of school after Zeno
      • known for a religios ode to Zeus
      • might have been his commentaries on Heraclitus physics that influenced Stoic physics
    • Chrysippus of Soli(cicilia, asia minor;230bce as headmaster;205bce death):
      • third head of the Stoic school
      • the great unifier, systematised the Stoic philosophy of its predecessors
  • middle(these started to inspire themselves from other philosophies):
    • Panaetius of Rhodes(185bce - 110bce;head of stoa in 128bce):
      • influenced Cicero
      • influenced by Plato and Aristotle
    • Posidonius of Apamea(siria;135bce - 51bce):
      • Cicero was his pupil
      • polymath contribuing to history, geography, astronomy, meteorology, biology and anthropology
      • famous contributions in psychology
  • late(first hand sources are available):
    • these stoics lose interest in physics and logic and focus on practical ethics
    • Seneca:
      • leters to St. Paul are forgeries
      • works to read:
        • Moral Letters(Epistulae Morales)(wide range of philosopical topics)
        • The Dialogues(Dialogi)
        • On Benefits(De Beneficiis)(political leadership)
        • On Mercy(De Clementia)(political leadership)
        • Natural Questions(Naturales Quaestiones)(physics and meteorology)
        • Pumpkinification(Apocolocytonsis)(satire on the deification of Emperor Claudius)
    • Epictetus(~50ce - ~130ce):
      • his pupil, Arrian might have written much of his texts
      • to read:
        • Discourses(Dissertationes)
        • Handbook(Enchiridion)
    • Marcus Aurelius(121ce - 180ce;roman emperor):
      • highly influenced by Epictetus’s Discourses
      • to read:
        • Mediatations
    • Cornutus(20ce):
      • known for Introduction to Greek Theology(Theologiae Graecae Compendium)
    • Musonius Rufus(~30ce - ~100ce;etruscan):
      • epictetus might have attended his classes
    • Hierocles and Cleomedes:
      • to read:

Decline of Stoicism:

  • much of their philosophy was taught orally, late stoics focusing on practical ethics, this means that even after a generation of stoics that never wrote anything down, the philosophy can dissapear

Logic:

if p, then q; p; therefore, q;

  • letters replace propositions(assertibles)
  • an assertible is a complete sayable:
    • type:
      • based on truth-value:
        • true
        • false
      • based on structure:
        • simple:
          • affirmation
          • negation
        • complex(it contains more than one simple assertible):
          • conditionals(if)
          • conjunctions(and - conjunction, or - disjunction)
          • negation(not)
      • based on modality:
        • possible
        • impossible
        • necessary
        • non-necessary
    • their true-value depends on when they where asserted
    • five basic arguments(indemonstrable):
      • if p, then q; p; therefore, q;
      • if p, then q; not q; therefore, not p;
      • not p and q; p; therefore, not q;
      • either p or q; p; therefore, not q;
      • either p or q; not q; therefore, not p;
    • if an argument is not of the basic form, use any or all of the four themata(ground-rules) to reduce the argument to one of the forms
    • aristotelic and stoic basic arguments complement one another(?!)

Language:

  • stoics are matherialists that claim that only physical bodies exist
  • the meaning or sense expressed by the physical act of speech is a sayable
  • three things linked together:
    • the thing signified(the concept: “Socrates the man”; an incorporeal, the sayable)
    • the thing signifying(the sound: “Socrates”; a body, the sound uttered)
    • the thing existing(the real object: “The man next to me, Socrates”; a body, the man next to me)
  • a sayable is expressed by an utterance that has meaning:
    • incomplete: ... is walking(further information needs to be asked)
    • complete: Socrates is walking

Epistemology:

  • an impression(on the soul; similar to how an seal makes an impression in a piece of wax) is presented to the mind as an proposition, the mind the accepts or rejects the proposition
  • empiricists in epistemology
  • they deny innate knowledge
  • stages:
    1. perception of an external event or state of affairs
    2. (sometimes) an almost involuntary and seemingly uncounscious value judgement about the content of the perception
    3. the presentation to the conscious mind of an impression in the form of a proposition that is composed of both the perceptual data and the unconscious value judgement
    4. assent or rejection of the proposition
  • how to discern between true or false impressions:
    • true impressions are adequate/cognitive
    • there are times when two impressions(one which is true and one which is not) are so similar that we can not discern the correct one, then further scrutiny(refer to the casual history of an impression) will reveal the correct one
  • you can assent a hole host of adequate impressions relating to the components of a system(cognition), but bringing all of these cognitions together into a systematic unit and understanding how they relate to one and other so that i have a proper grasp of the way in which the system functions all together, that si knowledge

Empiricism connections:

  • until now we could easily conclude that stoics are empiricists(all our knowledge derives from experience)
  • prolepses: fundamental assumptions, what is taken as agreed in advance; for mathematicians they are common notions; given what humans share in common physiologically with one another, many of them will occur in more or less everyone
  • we are not born with concepts of triangles, but we have innate ideas concerning molar notions
  • evidence suggests that stoics believed that individuals would naturally tend towards a virtous life and that all our all too common deviations from virtue are the product of either external influences or of faulty reasoning(this is a claim that we either have innate moral concepts or innate moral tendencies, or if born without innate concepts or tendencies, the natural course after birth will inevitably lead to the formation of an inclination thowards virtue)
  • cicero in On Ends says we are not born with innate moral concepts
  • seneca says Nature does supply us with the seeds of knowledge of goodnes, but not with the knowledge itself
