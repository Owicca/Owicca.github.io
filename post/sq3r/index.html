Effective Study - Francis P. Robinson

a typical student:

  • takes learning/reading as a chore
  • reads the textbox mechanically and if anything is hard to understand, it just keeps reading
  • reading is thought of in terms of quantity

effectivenes of “trial and error” student methods:

  • a test imediatelly after the learning session has 53% success in memory recollection

  • a test after multiple rereadings averages about 70% retention

  • reading 9, 8, 45, 6, 2, 4, 6, 8 just gives you the impressions that you learned the sequence because the numbers are familiar

  • efficient learning methods have been designed based on two helpers:

    • cues in the structure of the learning material:
      • speed up reading
      • improve comprehension of essential points
      • hint at quiz questions
      • cues:
        • textbook cues:
          • headings are the main points in the section
          • paragraphs contain as first or last sentence the main ideea
          • important words or ideeas are in italics or bold
          • the summaries at the start and/or end are important
          • lists of items are important:
            • 1, 2, 3
            • a), b), c), d)
            • “first…second…and finally”
            • “three kinds”
            • “four clauses”
          • graphs, chars, diagrams, maps
        • classroom lecture:
          • if the topics discusses are also found in the textbooks they are important
          • if the topics are not found, they complement the textbook
          • by analyzing the questions the teacher poses you can infer on what type quizes they will focus definitions, lists, applications, problems, interpretation
        • previous quizes:
          • you can see what are the chapters you need to review
          • you can see what is the source of the quizes: classroom lecture, textbook quizes
    • learning techniques discovered in experiments:
      • select and comprehend what is important:
        • quick preview:
          • sets up expectations for what the chapter will be about
        • set up questions about the section you will be reading
        • the most efficient size unit is variable between students:
          • familiar text can be longer
          • some students are not as trained to grasp larger ideeas
        • outlining and note-taking:
          • must be simple enough that it does not hinder the reading/learning process
      • retard forgetting

  • ways to retard forgetting:

    • nature of forgetting:
      • facts:
        1. not all material is forgoten at the same rate, not in the same way:
          • material we disagree will more easily be forgotten than material we agree with
          • important material is more easily retainable than unimportant one
          • memories gradually change to fit previous knowledge and tought patterns
        2. not all students forget at the same rate
    • methods of attacking the process of forgetting:
      1. interest and intent to remember:
        • the material needs to be relatable to students interests, if the material is not, the student needs to find a way to make it so
      2. selecting major points and key phrases:
        • make an outline of the material, this is the scheleton upon you hang new related information
      3. recitation:
        • understanding something does not mean you know it and you retained it
        • recitation tests act to retard forgeting, the earlier(right after reading), the better
        • what for this self-recitation take?:
          • read through a meaningful unit and then try self-recitation
          • read a headed section and jott down from memory a key phrase in the readers own words
          • turn the heading into a question and answer it by looking in the chapter
          • use questions provided by the teacher/author
          • start a discussion with a fellow reader
      4. distributed learning:
        • cramming will help short therm memory, but is very inefficient in the long term
        • spaced learning(read 1 every 3-4 hours) is ideal for long term retention of information
        • the distribution of sessions varies according to the purpose:
          1. clear comprehension or quick learning
          2. reviewing learning through review

  • development of survery q3r method:

    • steps:
      1. survey: for one minute survey the headings and chapter summaries
      2. question: make the headings into questions
      3. read: actively look for the answer to the question
      4. recite: look away from the source and try, in your own words to answer the question again, add at least one example
      5. review:
    • repeat 1, 2, 3, 4 for each succeding heading

  • effectivenes of the survey q3r method:

    • the author comes with anectodal evidence, but is the method really successful?

  • further details of the method:

    • some errors my happen, usually related to old studying habits interfering with the new technique
    • tips:
      1. survey:
        • this should be no longer than a minute
      2. and 3. reading to answer questions:
        • you have to counsciously want to answer the question, not to read line by line
        • the student must know what he must look for(the question)
        • look for it(reading)
        • organize its thinking on the topic(learning)
      3. reciting:
        • be sure that at the end of every headed section you answered the heading question:
          • don’t just mentally review: write it out, verbalize it, draw it
        • note-taking:
          • no notes should be written untill the whole headed section is read
          • the notes should be written from memory
          • the notes should be in the students words, very short sentences or simple words
      4. review:
        • not more than 5 minutes
        • look over the main outline
        • work rate should be above 150(time in minutes / words in chapter)
